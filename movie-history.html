Movie History & Behind the Scenes

Lilo & Stitch (2002)

Release Date: June 21, 2002

Directors: Chris Sanders & Dean DeBlois (directorial debuts)

Budget: $80 million

Box Office: $273.1 million worldwide

Studio: Walt Disney Feature Animation

Setting: Kauai, Hawaii

Awards: Nominated for Academy Award for Best Animated Feature

Development Timeline

1985 - The Birth of Stitch

Chris Sanders first conceptualized Stitch for an unsuccessful children's book about a creature living in a forest. The character would remain dormant for over a decade.

1997 - Disney Retreat

At a Disney executive retreat, Thomas Schumacher suggested creating "the Dumbo for our generation" - a smaller, more intimate animated film compared to Disney's big-budget productions.

Original Concept - Kansas Forest

Initially, Stitch was supposed to crash-land in a forest in rural Kansas and interact only with woodland animals. This concept was later changed to feature human characters.

Location Change - Hawaii

Sanders relocated the story to Hawaii after glancing at a map on his wall. This decision would fundamentally reshape the entire movie and introduce the central theme of 'ohana.

2001 - Post-9/11 Changes

A major action sequence involving hijacking a Boeing 747 and flying through downtown Honolulu was completely reworked to use Jumba's spaceship flying through Kauai's mountains instead.

üé® Revolutionary Animation

  • Watercolor Backgrounds: First Disney film in decades to use watercolor painted backgrounds instead of traditional gouache
  • Unique Art Style: Based on Chris Sanders' personal drawing style rather than traditional Disney house style
  • Florida Studio: Primarily produced at Disney's Florida animation studio, away from Burbank headquarters
  • Limited Budget Effects: Creative use of shadows and lighting to work within budget constraints

üå∫ Hawaiian Culture & Music

  • 'Ohana Concept: The Hawaiian concept of extended family became the film's central theme after a research trip to Kauai
  • Elvis Presley Music: Extensive use of Elvis songs throughout the film, reflecting Lilo's character
  • Authentic Voices: Tia Carrere (Honolulu native) and Jason Scott Lee (Hawaiian descent) helped rewrite dialogue in proper Hawaiian dialect
  • Cultural Accuracy: Animation team visited Kauai for research and cultural understanding
